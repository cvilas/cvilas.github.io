Visit to the National Air and Space Museum, Washington D.C., May 2005.

There are images from my visit to the Smithsonian National Air and Space Museum's Steven F. Udvar-Hazy Center near Washington Dulles International Airport.


Photo 1: This is the Concorde F-BVFA, first of the Air France supersonic jet transport aircrafts.


Photo 2: The museum hanger is so crowded with aircrafts that it is difficult to find a spot from where the entire concorde can be captured on film without obstruction. That's a shame, because it is a very sleek and breath-takingly beautiful aircraft.


Photo 3: Concorde from under its belly.


Photo 4: Boeing B-29-45-MO Superfortress "Enola Gay". On August 6, 1945, Colonel Paul W. Tibbets, Jr., in command of the Enola Gay, dropped the first atomic bomb on Hiroshima, Japan. More information


Photo 5: This is the Space Shuttle Enterprise, a test vehicle not equipped for space flight. This vehicle was used for approach and landing tests. More information



Space Shuttle: The First 20 Years - The Astronauts' Experiences in Their Own Words (Hardcover) by DK Publishing (ISBN: 0789484250). Bought this one for $40 at the museum store. I later found that this is available for $15 at Amazon. That doesn't hurt at all if a part of the sales contribute to the maintainence of artifacts in this museum. Entry to this museum is free, and that's more than what a person like me could ever wish for. Reading this book inspired me to learn more about NASA's research into manned space flight. I ended up getting "Space Shuttle: The History of the National Space Transportation System : The First 100 Missions" (ISBN: 0963397451) by Dennis R. Jenkins. This book covers the technical history behind research into re-usable space vehicles. This is a wonderful resource that any engineer interested in learning the history of space vehicles would love to have.

Photo 8: The sleek and mean-looking Lockheed SR-71A "Blackbird". Built by Lockheed's clandestine "Skunk Works" division, this reconnaissance aircraft remained in service until March 1990. On its way to the museum, it arrived at the Washington Dulles International airport after a record-setting flight from Los Angeles that took 1 hour, 20 seconds, averaging 2124 kmph. More information


Photo 9: The right side rudder of the SR-71 on display.


Photo 10: "After the Air Force began to operate the SR-71, it acquired the official name Blackbird - for the special black paint that covered the airplane. This paint was formulated to absorb radar signals, to radiate some of the tremendous airframe heat generated by air friction, and to camouflage the aircraft against the dark sky at high altitudes."


Photo 11: A scaled model of the mars Pathfinder lander, and the Sojourner rover.


Photo 12: UNIVAC 1232 computer, used by U.S. Air Force Satellite Control Facility in Sunnyvale, California. The 1232 also supported Space Shuttle missions.


Photo 13: An android on display at the Space Hanger.

Apart from the various aircrafts and spacecrafts on display, the museum also has various related artifacts, such as missiles, space suits, enabling technologies such as old mainframe computers, and an IMAX theatre.
