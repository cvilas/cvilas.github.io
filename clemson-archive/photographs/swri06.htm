OCTOR Project Demonstration, DARPA Biodynotics Program
Southwest Research Institute, San Antonio, TX, April 6, 2006

Photo 1: OCTOR test site at SWRI.


Photo 2: The arm consists of 10 actuators: 9 mckibben muscles + 1 base motor. Encoders at the base enable estimation of the 3D shape of the arm. Other sensors include 9 IR distance sensors, 6 tactile sensors, and a tip camera. Like suckers on an octopus arm, suction cups can be mounted along the length of the octarm and activated through vacuum lines.


Photo 3: The pneumatic/electrical rotary union allows rotation of the manipulator about its axis - a useful feature for tasks such as twisting a door knob or turning a wrench.


Photo 4: Octarm demonstrates manipulation utilizing a pneumatic sucker.


Photo 5: Demonstration of 'whole arm manipulation'.


Photo 6: Demonstration of master-slave tele-operation. The 'slave' arm on the talon follows the motion of a remote 'master' arm directly manipulated by a human operator. Advantages of this mode of operation include (a) fine manipulation difficult to achieve using a joystick alone as the operator input device, and (2), 'force feedback' that can be felt by the operator as the slave interacts with the objects in its environment.


Photo 7: Tele-operation demo.


Photo 8: Alternate sucker designs were demonstrated.


Photo 9: Fairly clear who are under maximum pressure here.


Photo 10: Tele-operation. The laptop displays video feed from the tip camera and the shape of the arm.


Photo 11: Drag test. The arm could pull 135 pounds without tearing apart.
