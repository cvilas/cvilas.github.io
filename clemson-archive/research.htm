Full paper (490 kB PDF) 		Vision-Based Tracking for Unmanned Aerial Vehicles
Team: Vilas Chitrakaran, Hariprasad Kannan
Advisors: Dr Darren Dawson, Dr Matthew Feemster

This research presents the design of a vision-based controller for an underactuated, unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) equipped with a pan-tilt camera unit (PTCU) to achieve the objective of following a leader vehicle autonomously. The approach we developed is also applied, with a few modifications, to the problem of trajectory tracking, where the desired trajectory is described as a sequence of images taken, for example, by the on-board camera during a previous flight.


Full paper (224 kB PDF)
More information 		Vision Assisted Autonomous Landing of an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle
Team: Vilas Chitrakaran, Jian Chen
Advisors: Dr Darren Dawson, Dr Matthew Feemster

A strategy for autonomous landing maneuver using position information obtained from a single monocular camera on-board an underactuated, unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) is presented. Although the UAV is underactuated in translational control inputs (i.e., a lift force can only be produced), the proposed controller is shown to achieve globally uniform ultimate boundedness (GUUB) in position regulation error during the landing approach.


Full paper (900 kB PDF)
More information 		Euclidean Position Estimation of Features on an Object Using a Single Camera: A Lyapunov-Based Approach
Team: Vilas Chitrakaran, Jian Chen
Advisors: Dr Darren Dawson, Dr Warren Dixon

The recovery of the structure of an object from its motion is a mainstream research problem with significant potential impact for applications such as autonomous vehicle/robotic guidance, navigation and control. In this paper, a unique nonlinear estimation strategy is presented to address the SFM problem. Homography-based techniques are used in the development of the object kinematics, while Lyapunov design methods are utilized in the synthesis of the adaptive estimator.

My documentation 		OCTOR: Soft Robotic Manipulators
Team: Bryan Jones, Vilas Chitrakaran, Will McMahan, Vikram Iyengar
Advisors: Dr Ian Walker, Dr Darren Dawson

The objective of this research is to develop a new class of biologically inspired soft robot manipulators resembling biological trunks and tentacles. These robots will be significantly more versatile and adaptable than conventional robots, capable of adaptive and dynamic manipulation in unstructured environments. This research is sponsored by the Biodynatics program of the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA).

More information 		Identification of a Moving Object's Velocity with a Fixed Camera
Team: J.Chen, W.E.Dixon, Vilas Chitrakaran, Prakash Chawda
Advisor: Dr Darren Dawson

Using images of a moving object from a single fixed camera, the homography between image coordinates of various feature points on the moving object are computed relative to image coordinates of the same feature points when the object is at a static reference position. Decomposition of homography yields Euclidean information about the motion of the object. A continuous Lyapunov based estimator uses this information to asymptotically identify the 6 DOF translational and rotational velocity of the moving object.


CCA 2001 paper (417 kB PDF)
More Information 		The Robotic Platform
Team: Markus Loffler, Vilas Chitrakaran, Erkan Zergeroglu, Ankur Bhargava
Advisor: Dr Darren Dawson

The Robotic Platform is an open software development platform for robotic applications. It includes hardware interfacing, servo control, trajectory generation, 3D simulation, a graphical user interface, and a math library. As opposed to distributed solutions, the Robotic Platform is the first system that implements all these components on a single hardware platform (a standard PC), with a single programming language (C++), and on a single operating system (the QNX Real-Time Platform) while guaranteeing hard real-time performance.

Nonlinear Dynamics of High-speed Yarn Unwinding
Team: Vilas Chitrakaran, James Gibert
Advisor: Dr Eric M. Austin

The primary goal of this research is to understand the complex relationships between various parameters such as yarn drag (friction) over the package, yarn evenness, hairiness and tension during high-speed unwinding. This understanding will allow us to develop refined mathematical models and enable textile engineers to use computer-aided design to optimize the productivity of high-speed yarn transport systems. The system is developed using QMotor and the QNX4.25 Realtime Operating System.
